Tax rates and income brackets for individuals

Canadian federal, provincial, and territorial tax rates and income brackets for current and previous tax years.

How rates and brackets work

The tax rates are split up into income brackets. A different tax rate applies to each bracket, but only to the income within that bracket.

The taxes you pay are based on which one or more of the brackets your income falls into. The higher tax rate does not apply to all your income.

The same brackets are used for all types of income, such as:

  • Employment income
  • Occasional earnings
  • Varied income (such as capital gains or rental income)

You determine your federal tax rate and provincial or territorial tax rate separately. Your combined tax rate would be the sum of the two individual rates but is not the rate used to calculate your taxes.

Adjustments to income brackets

Each year, income bracket thresholds increase based on inflation. Certain other personal income tax and benefit amounts are also adjusted based on inflation.

    Understanding your tax amount

    The taxes you owe are calculated using your taxable income.

    Federal and provincial or territorial taxes (except Quebec) are calculated the same way. They apply in addition to each other.

    Example 1: Calculation for 2024 provincial tax on taxable income in the first bracket

    Example:

    Giovanni's taxable income was $45,000. He was living in British Columbia on December 31, 2024.

    British Columbia income tax rates for 2024
    Tax rate Taxable income bracket threshold Bracket
    5.06% on the portion of taxable income that is $47,937 or less, plus First
    7.7% on the portion of taxable income over $47,937 up to $95,875, plus Second
    10.5% on the portion of taxable income over $95,875 up to $110,076, plus Third
    12.29% on the portion of taxable income over $110,076 up to $133,664, plus Fourth
    14.7% on the portion of taxable income over $133,664 up to $181,232, plus Fifth
    16.8% on the portion of taxable income over $181,232 up to $252,752, plus Sixth
    20.5% on the portion of taxable income over $252,752 Seventh

    Because he made less than $47,937, his income falls into the first tax bracket. The 2024 tax rate for this bracket is 5.06%.

    When Giovanni does his taxes, the provincial taxes he owes on his income are calculated like this:

    • Giovanni is taxed at 5.06% rate on his income of $45,000
      • 5.06% ltmuiplied by $45,000 =equals $2,277

    Giovanni's total provincial tax is $2,277. He can apply credits and additional deductions to lower this amount.

    Giovanni still needs to calculate his federal tax as well.

    Example 2: Calculation for 2024 federal tax on taxable income in the third bracket

    Example:

    Maria's taxable income was $115,000.

    Federal income tax rates for 2024
    Tax rate Taxable income bracket threshold Bracket
    15% on the portion of taxable income that is $55,867 or less, plus First
    20.5% on the portion of taxable income over $55,867 up to $111,733, plus Second
    26% on the portion of taxable income over $111,733 up to $173,205, plus Third
    29% on the portion of taxable income over $173,205 up to $246,752, plus Fourth
    33% on the portion of taxable income over $246,752 Fifth

    Because she made between $111,733 to $173,205, her income falls into the first, second, and third tax brackets.

    Maria will not pay taxes at the 26% rate on the whole $115,000, but instead will pay a different tax rate for each level of her income based on the federal income tax brackets.

    When Maria does her taxes, the federal taxes she owes on her income are calculated like this:

    1. Maria is taxed at 15% rate on the first $55,867 (first bracket)
      • 15% ×multiplied by $55,867 =equals $8,380
    2. Maria is taxed at 20.5% rate for the income between $55,867 and $111,733 (second bracket)
      • 20.5% ×multiplied by ($111,733 −minus $55,867) =equals $11,452
    3. Maria is taxed at 26% rate for the income between $111,733 and $115,000 (third bracket)
      • 26% ×multiplied by ($115,000 −minus $111,733) =equals $849
    4. Maria calculates the total federal taxes on her $115,000 taxable income by adding the results from calculations 1 to 3
      • $8,380 +plus $11,452 +plus $849 =equals $20,681

    Maria's total federal tax is $20,681. She can apply credits and additional deductions to lower this amount.

    Maria still needs to calculate her provincial or territorial tax as well.

    If your location differs from the location of your income source

    There are additional considerations if the location where you reside is different from the location of your income source.

    If you live in one province or territory and work in a different province or territory

    Most employers will consider you a resident in the region which you work when deducting provincial or territorial tax from your pay.

    You are also required to pay provincial or territorial taxes where you reside.

    For example, an Ontario resident working in Quebec will generally have their pay taxed following Quebec’s provincial taxes. They must also pay Ontario provincial taxes when filing their Income Tax and Benefit Return.

    If you live outside of Canada but have income from Canada

    Depending on your residency status, the type of income you incurred, and the type of return being filed, the taxes you owe will vary.

    For example, you may have to pay a surtax instead of a provincial or territorial tax.

    For more information, refer to: International and non-resident taxes

    Reducing your tax amount

    You can reduce the amount of tax you owe to the CRA at the end of the tax year by claiming amounts on your tax return and increasing deductions from your income.

    • Claim your deductions, credits, and expenses

      You may be eligible to claim various deductions, credits, and expenses to reduce the amount of tax you owe.

      Example of a federal tax credit:

      Basic personal amount

      The basic personal amount is a federal non-refundable tax credit that is adjusted yearly based on inflation.

      It provides a partial reduction to anyone with a taxable income above the amount. In general, it provides a full reduction to anyone with a taxable income below the amount, meaning they do not have to pay federal income tax.

      Refer to: Basic personal amount

      For information about adjustments to the amount, refer to: Footnote 1, Basic personal amount

      For all deduction types, refer to: All deductions, credits, and expenses

    • Increase your tax deductions

      Increasing deductions from your income source can help you avoid owing at the end of the tax year.

      Increase deductions from your paycheque

      Generally, most employers will deduct both federal income tax and provincial or territorial income tax directly from your pay. You can choose to have more tax deducted from your pay by completing a TD1 form and providing it to your employer.

      Refer to: Form TD1, Personal Tax Credits Returns

      If you are an employer, refer to: Increasing income tax deducted at source

      Increase deductions from your CPP payments

      Taxes are not automatically deducted from your CPP payments but you can ask that federal tax be deducted.

      For more information, refer to: Deducting federal income tax from your CPP payments

    Taxable income

    Taxable income is line 26000 of your Income Tax and Benefit Return. It is your income after various deductions have been applied.

    If you live in Quebec on December 31

    If you live in Quebec on December 31 of the tax year, you will calculate your provincial taxes by completing a separate provincial tax return for Revenu Québec.

    Refer to: Revenu Québec
